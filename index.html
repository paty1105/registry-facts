Section 01 — The Basics

What is sex offender registration, really?

Sex offender registration laws require people convicted of certain sexual offenses to report personal information to the government on an ongoing basis — and in many states, to remain on a public website for years or even a lifetime.

Most people assume registration means a name and address on a website. That's part of it. But in most states today, registration involves much more than that — and the requirements have grown significantly over the past two decades.

Registration is not a one-time event. In most states, it is an ongoing system of requirements that can span decades — or never end at all.

What registration typically requires today

Requirements vary significantly by state, but across the country, registered people are commonly required to appear in person at regular intervals — quarterly in many states — to update their information with law enforcement. They must report every address where they spend the night, all vehicles they drive, their employers, their schools, and in most states, every email address, username, and social media account they use.

Beyond reporting, many states prohibit registered people from living within 1,000 to 2,500 feet of schools, parks, playgrounds, or bus stops. Their names, photographs, and home addresses appear on publicly accessible websites. In most states, their driver's licenses are marked. Since 2016, those subject to federal SORNA requirements must carry a passport stamped with a notice visible to foreign border agents.

Why this matters: When courts first upheld these laws, most of today's requirements didn't exist. The legal question now being asked — in courtrooms across the country — is whether the system has expanded so far beyond its origins that it warrants a fresh look.

Section 02 — History

The registry of today is not the registry of 2003

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in 2003 that sex offender registration laws were constitutional — but the system it reviewed looked very different from what most states require today. That case, Smith v. Doe, evaluated Alaska's registry as it existed then: a relatively modest set of requirements that included an annual mailed update and limited public access to information.

In the two decades since, state legislatures and Congress have layered requirement upon requirement onto that original framework. The result is a system that many legal scholars argue the 2003 Court never actually approved — because it never saw it.

The legal significance: Courts evaluate registration laws against the system that existed when they ruled. As requirements have expanded, a growing number of federal courts are reconsidering whether those expansions can still be called civil regulation — or whether they have become punishment in all but name.

How we got here: a brief timeline

1994

Jacob Wetterling Act

First federal law requiring states to maintain registries of people convicted of sex crimes against children. Focused on law enforcement access — not public websites.

1996

Megan's Law

Required states to make registry information available to the public. Named after Megan Kanka, murdered by a neighbor with prior convictions. Created the modern public notification model.

2003

Smith v. Doe — Supreme Court Rules

The Court upheld Alaska's registry law as civil regulation, not criminal punishment. This decision shaped every legal challenge since — but the registry at that time was far less extensive than today's.

2006

Adam Walsh Act / SORNA

Created a national three-tier framework and dramatically expanded requirements: in-person reporting, internet identifiers, travel notifications, passport markings. Many states adopted parts of this framework.

Now

A patchwork — and an ongoing legal debate

Each state has different rules. Some are more expansive, some less. Courts in multiple circuits are actively questioning whether the current system remains constitutional.

Section 03 — Behavioral Science

What does the research say about risk?

One of the most common assumptions underlying the registry is that people who commit sexual offenses will inevitably do so again. The research on this is more nuanced — and more hopeful — than most public debate acknowledges.

Long-term studies consistently find that the majority of people convicted of sexual offenses do not commit new sexual offenses after release. Recidivism rates vary depending on how "recidivism" is defined — a critical distinction we explore in the next section — but studies measuring new sexual convictions typically find rates in the range of 5 to 15 percent over follow-up periods of five to fifteen years.

The desistance finding: Risk is not static. Multiple large studies have found that the longer a person remains offense-free, the lower their risk of future offending becomes — a pattern researchers call desistance. A person ten years out from their offense has a measurably different risk profile than someone recently released.

The stranger danger gap

The public notification model underlying the registry — the idea that neighbors need to know who lives near them — is largely built around the assumption that sexual offenders are strangers who pose a threat to people they don't know. The research consistently challenges this assumption.

Studies on the relationship between offenders and victims find that the majority of sexual offenses, particularly those involving children, are committed by someone the victim already knows — a family member, a family friend, a trusted adult with legitimate access. This does not mean strangers pose no risk. It means the registry's architecture was built around a threat pattern that does not reflect how most sexual harm actually occurs.

The structural limitation: Because registries only list people who have already been convicted, they cannot include first-time offenders — the population responsible for the majority of new sexual offenses. This is a factual constraint that researchers consistently note when evaluating what the registry can and cannot accomplish.

Section 04 — Understanding the Data

The difference between a paperwork violation and a new crime

This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of registry data — and one of the most important distinctions to understand when reading news reports or policy debates about sex offender "reoffending."

When a registered person is arrested, it is often reported simply as a sex offender reoffending. But there are several very different things that can trigger an arrest for someone on the registry, and they are not remotely equivalent.

In most states, failing to update an address, forgetting to report a new vehicle, spending a night away from home without notifying authorities, or failing to report a new email address are all criminal offenses. These are called administrative or technical violations — and they are counted alongside new sexual assault allegations in many "recidivism" statistics.

When a registrant is arrested, it could be for either of these — and they are not the same thing

Both are crimes under state law. The point is not that paperwork violations are harmless. The point is that combining them with new sexual offense allegations in a single recidivism statistic tells us something very different than separating them — and that difference matters enormously for honest policy evaluation.

When lawmakers, researchers, or media reports cite reoffending rates, the question to always ask is: what exactly did they reoffend with? Responsible research separates these categories. Responsible policy should too.

Section 05 — Legal Landscape

What have courts said?

The legal debate over registration laws began almost as soon as the laws themselves did — and it has accelerated in recent years. Here are the key decisions, explained in plain English.

The foundational case is Smith v. Doe (2003), in which the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that Alaska's registry was civil regulation rather than criminal punishment, allowing it to apply retroactively to people convicted before the law existed. That ruling has shaped every legal challenge since. But as the requirements have expanded dramatically beyond what existed in 2003, courts are increasingly asking whether the original analysis still holds.

The central legal question: Has the registry changed enough since 2003 that the original Supreme Court ruling no longer covers what states are actually doing — and has it crossed the line from regulation into punishment?

Smith v. Doe
U.S. Supreme Court · 2003

The foundational case. The Court ruled Alaska's registry was civil regulation, not punishment. This allowed retroactive application. But the registry reviewed in 2003 was far less expansive than what most states require today.

Doe v. Snyder
6th Circuit Court of Appeals · 2016

A landmark departure from 2003. The court ruled Michigan's expanded requirements had become so burdensome they constituted punishment — and could not apply to people convicted before those rules existed.

State v. Letalvis Cofield
North Carolina Supreme Court · 2023

North Carolina's highest court struck down satellite-based monitoring for some registrants as an unreasonable search under the Fourth Amendment. Courts are increasingly scrutinizing modern registry surveillance tools.

Does v. Wasden & others
Multiple federal circuits · Ongoing

Challenges to expanded internet reporting, travel restrictions, and residency requirements are working through federal courts — arguing the system has grown beyond what any court has approved.

Section 06 — Common Questions

Questions people actually search for

These are the questions families, registrants, journalists, students, and citizens most often ask when they first encounter this topic. We've answered each one as plainly and honestly as the research allows.

Does the sex offender registry actually make communities safer? +

The research on this is genuinely mixed. Some studies find modest public safety benefits in certain contexts. Others find little to no measurable reduction in sexual offending linked to registry laws. What researchers broadly agree on is that the evidence is not as clear-cut as the policy assumes — and that the system has grown significantly since it was first evaluated.

Do sex offenders always reoffend? +

No — and this is one of the most persistent myths the research pushes back on. Long-term studies consistently find that most people convicted of sexual offenses do not commit new sexual offenses after release. Rates vary by study and how recidivism is defined, but the common assumption of near-certain reoffense is not supported by the evidence. Risk also decreases significantly over time.

Can someone get off the sex offender registry? +

It depends entirely on the state. Some states have petition processes allowing people to apply for removal after a certain number of offense-free years. Others have lifetime registration with no removal option. Federal law (SORNA) sets minimum standards but states can be more restrictive. An attorney familiar with your state's laws is the right resource for individual situations.

What happens if someone misses a reporting deadline? +

Missing a reporting requirement — even something like failing to report a new email address or a temporary lodging stay — can result in a new criminal charge in most states. These are called administrative or technical violations. They are real crimes under state law, but they are paperwork offenses, not new sexual assaults. This distinction matters enormously when evaluating registry statistics.

Most abuse is committed by strangers, right? +

Research consistently shows the opposite. The majority of sexual offenses, particularly against children, are committed by someone the victim already knows — a family member, trusted adult, acquaintance, or someone with legitimate access. The registry's public website model is largely built around stranger danger, which research suggests does not reflect how most sexual harm actually occurs.

Is the registry the same in every state? +

No. Requirements vary significantly. Some states require quarterly in-person reporting; others annual. Some have residency restrictions within 2,500 feet of schools; others have none. Duration ranges from 10 years to lifetime. Federal law sets a minimum floor, but states can and do exceed it substantially.

How does this affect families and children of registrants? +

Research on this question is growing. Residency restrictions can force families to relocate or live apart. Public registry listings affect employment, housing, and community relationships for family members who share a home with a registrant. Children of registrants often face social consequences at school. These collateral effects are increasingly part of the policy conversation, though they remain understudied.

Why is this website not advocacy? +

Because the evidence doesn't belong to one side. This site follows the same principle as the Registry Research Institute: evidence before opinion. We're not arguing for or against the registry. We're explaining what the research shows so you can form your own informed view. Where science is mixed or uncertain, we say so clearly.
