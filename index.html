Section 01 — Basics

What is sex offender registration, really?

Sex offender registration laws require people convicted of certain sexual offenses to report personal information to the government on an ongoing basis — and in many states, to remain on a public website for years or even life.

Most people assume registration means your name and address are listed online. That's part of it. But in most states today, registration also involves much more.

Registration is not a one-time event. In most states, it is an ongoing set of requirements that can last decades — or a lifetime.

What registration typically requires today

Requirements vary by state, but across the country, registered people are often required to:

Reporting obligations

• Appear in person regularly (quarterly, semi-annually, or annually)

• Report every address — including temporary stays

• Report vehicles, employers, and schools

• Report travel plans — sometimes days in advance

• Report all internet usernames and email addresses

Restrictions & notifications

• Cannot live near schools, parks, or bus stops in many states

• Cannot be present in certain locations

• Name, photo, and address on public website

• Driver's license may be marked

• U.S. passport marked with a notice

Why this matters: When courts first upheld these laws, most of today's requirements didn't exist. The question researchers and courts are now examining is whether the system has grown beyond what was originally justified.

Section 02 — History

The registry of today is not the registry of 2003

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in 2003 that sex offender registration laws were legal — but the system it reviewed looked very different from what most states require today. Here's what changed.

Requirement 2003 (When Court Ruled) Today (Many States)
Address reporting Annual mailed update In-person quarterly or more often
Public notification Limited — some states, by request Automatic public website with photo, address, workplace
Internet identifiers Not required Required — email, usernames, social media
Travel reporting Not required in most states Required — international travel, sometimes domestic
Residency restrictions Rare Common — often within 1,000–2,500 ft of schools, parks
ID marking Not used Required in many states — driver's license, passport
Temporary lodging Not required Must report any stay away from home address

The Supreme Court's 2003 ruling was based on the system at that time. Researchers and legal scholars are now asking whether the system has changed enough that it should be re-evaluated.

A brief timeline of how we got here

1994

Jacob Wetterling Act

First federal law requiring states to maintain registries of people convicted of sex crimes against children. Focused on law enforcement access — not public websites.

1996

Megan's Law

Required states to make registry information available to the public. Named after Megan Kanka, murdered by a neighbor with prior convictions. Created the modern public notification system.

2003

Smith v. Doe — Supreme Court Rules

The Court upheld Alaska's registry law, ruling it was civil regulation — not criminal punishment. This decision shaped every legal challenge since. But the registry at that time was far less extensive than today's.

2006

Adam Walsh Act / SORNA

Created a national framework with three tiers. Dramatically expanded requirements: in-person reporting, internet identifiers, travel notifications, passport markings. Many states adopted parts of this framework.

Now

An evolving system — state by state

Each state has different rules. Some are more expansive, some less. The result is a patchwork of requirements — and ongoing legal challenges in courts across the country.

Section 03 — Behavioral Science

What does the research say about risk?

One of the most common assumptions about the registry is that it exists because people who commit sexual offenses will always do so again. The research on this is more complicated — and more nuanced — than most people realize.

~5–15%
Typical sexual recidivism rate in long-term studies, when measured as new sexual convictions
↓ with age
Risk of reoffense decreases significantly as people get older — a pattern researchers call desistance
Varies
Risk is not the same for everyone — individual factors matter significantly

Important context: These numbers come from studies that define recidivism as a new sexual conviction or charge. Studies that count any rearrest — including paperwork violations — show much higher numbers. The definition matters enormously.

What researchers have found

Desistance — risk changes over time

Multiple large studies have found that the risk of sexual reoffense decreases significantly the longer a person remains offense-free. This is called desistance. A person who has been offense-free for 10 years has a substantially lower risk profile than someone recently released.

Research suggests that lifetime registration may not match the actual risk trajectory for many individuals.

Individual risk assessment

Actuarial risk tools — structured scoring instruments — can assess an individual's risk more accurately than broad offense categories. Researchers argue these tools are more reliable than assuming all people on the registry pose equal risk.

Many registration tiers are based on offense type rather than individual risk assessment.

Stranger danger vs. reality

Research consistently shows that most sexual offenses — particularly against children — are committed by someone known to the victim, not a stranger. The registry's public notification model is largely designed around stranger-danger scenarios.

First-time offenders

Because registries only list people who have already been convicted, they cannot include first-time offenders — the population responsible for most new sexual offenses. This is a structural limitation that researchers note when evaluating registry effectiveness.

None of this means risk is zero. It means risk varies, changes over time, and can be assessed individually — and that public policy benefits from understanding those distinctions.

Section 04 — Understanding the Data

The difference between a paperwork violation and a new crime

This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of the registry — and one of the most important to understand when reading news reports or policy debates.

When someone on the registry gets arrested, it is often reported as a "sex offender reoffending." But there are actually several very different things that can trigger an arrest for a registered person.

When a registrant is arrested, it could be for any of these — and they are not the same thing

Administrative / Paperwork Violation
  • Didn't update address in time
  • Forgot to report a new vehicle
  • Stayed somewhere overnight without reporting it
  • Missed an in-person check-in appointment
  • Didn't report a new email address
  • Travel not reported in advance
≠
New Sexual Offense Allegation
  • Allegation of a new sexual assault
  • New abuse allegation
  • New contact offense
  • New exploitation offense

These are both illegal. The point is not that paperwork violations don't matter. The point is that they should be counted separately when evaluating whether the registry is reducing sexual victimization — because they are measuring very different things.

Why this matters for public debate

When lawmakers or media reports say "registrants reoffended at X rate," the question to ask is: what did they reoffend with? If that number includes people who missed a reporting appointment, it tells us something very different than if it only counts new sexual assault allegations.

Responsible reporting on registry data separates these categories. The Registry Research Institute tracks how states define and report these distinctions — because accurate categories lead to better policy.

Section 05 — Legal Landscape

What have courts said?

The legal debate over registration laws has continued in courts since the 1990s. Here are the key decisions explained in plain English.

Smith v. Doe
U.S. Supreme Court · 2003

The foundational case. The Court ruled Alaska's registry was not "punishment" — it was civil regulation. This allowed it to apply retroactively. But the registry reviewed in 2003 was far less expansive than what most states require today.

Doe v. Snyder
6th Circuit Court of Appeals · 2016

A federal appeals court ruled that Michigan's expanded registry requirements were so burdensome they had become punishment — and could not be applied to people convicted before those rules existed. A significant departure from the 2003 framework.

State v. Letalvis Cofield (NC)
North Carolina Supreme Court · 2023

North Carolina's highest court struck down that state's satellite-based monitoring program for some registrants, ruling it constituted an unreasonable search under the Fourth Amendment. Courts are increasingly scrutinizing modern registry tools.

Does v. Wasden
9th Circuit · Ongoing challenges

Multiple cases in federal circuits are challenging expanded internet reporting, travel restrictions, and residency requirements — arguing that the system has grown beyond what courts originally approved.

The legal question being asked in courts today: Has the registry changed enough since 2003 that the original Supreme Court ruling no longer covers what states are actually doing?

Section 06 — Common Questions

Questions people actually search for

These are the questions families, registrants, journalists, and curious citizens most often ask. Answered plainly.

Does the sex offender registry actually make communities safer? +

The research on this is genuinely mixed. Some studies find modest public safety benefits in certain contexts. Others find little to no measurable reduction in sexual offending linked to registry laws. What researchers broadly agree on is that the evidence is not as clear-cut as the policy assumes — and that the system has grown significantly since it was first evaluated.

Do sex offenders always reoffend? +

No — and this is one of the most persistent myths the research pushes back on. Long-term studies consistently find that most people convicted of sexual offenses do not commit new sexual offenses after release. Rates vary by study and how recidivism is defined, but the common assumption of near-certain reoffense is not supported by the evidence. Risk also decreases significantly over time.

Can someone get off the sex offender registry? +

It depends entirely on the state. Some states have petition processes that allow people to apply for removal after a certain number of offense-free years. Others have lifetime registration with no removal option. Federal law (SORNA) sets minimum standards but states can be more restrictive. An attorney familiar with your state's specific laws is the right resource for individual situations.

What happens if someone misses a reporting deadline? +

Missing a reporting requirement — even for something like failing to report a new email address or a temporary lodging stay — can result in a new criminal charge in most states. These are called administrative or technical violations. They are real crimes under state law, but they are paperwork offenses, not new sexual assaults. This distinction matters when evaluating registry statistics.

Most abuse is committed by strangers, right? +

Actually no — research consistently shows the opposite. The majority of sexual offenses, particularly against children, are committed by someone the victim knows: a family member, trusted adult, acquaintance, or someone with legitimate access. The registry's public website model is largely built around stranger danger, which research suggests does not reflect how most sexual harm actually occurs.

Is the registry the same in every state? +

No. Requirements vary significantly. Some states require quarterly in-person reporting; others are annual. Some have residency restrictions within 2,500 feet of schools; others have none. Duration ranges from 10 years to lifetime depending on the offense and the state. Federal law sets a minimum floor, but states can and do exceed it substantially.

Why is this website not advocacy? +

Because the evidence doesn't belong to one side. This site follows the same principle as the Registry Research Institute: evidence before opinion. We're not arguing for or against the registry. We're explaining what the research shows so you can form your own informed view. Where science is mixed or uncertain, we say so.
