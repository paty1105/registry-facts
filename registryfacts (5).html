Section 01 — The Basics

What is sex offender registration, really?

Families across the country navigate registration requirements that have grown dramatically since 2003.

Sex offender registration laws require people convicted of certain sexual offenses to report personal information to the government on an ongoing basis — and in many states, to remain on a public website for years or even a lifetime.

Most people assume registration means a name and address on a website. That's part of it. But in most states today, registration involves quarterly in-person reporting, disclosure of every email address and social media account, advance reporting of travel, and restrictions on where a person may live.

Registration is not a one-time event. In most states, it is an ongoing system of requirements that can span decades — or never end at all.

Section 02 — History

The registry of today is not the registry of 2003

The Supreme Court's 2003 ruling reviewed a system far less expansive than what most states require today.

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in 2003 that sex offender registration laws were constitutional. But the system it evaluated — an annual mailed update and limited public access — looks nothing like what most states require today.

In the two decades since, state legislatures and Congress have layered requirement upon requirement onto that original framework. Many legal scholars argue the 2003 Court never approved the current system — because it never saw it.

The legal significance: A growing number of federal courts are reconsidering whether the expanded requirements still qualify as civil regulation — or whether they have become punishment in all but name.

A Brief Timeline

1994

Jacob Wetterling Act

First federal law requiring states to maintain registries. Focused on law enforcement access — not public websites.

1996

Megan's Law

Required states to make registry information publicly available. Created the modern public notification model.

2003

Smith v. Doe — Supreme Court Rules

The Court upheld Alaska's registry as civil regulation. But that registry was far less expansive than today's.

2006

Adam Walsh Act / SORNA

Created a national three-tier framework. Dramatically expanded requirements: in-person reporting, internet identifiers, travel notifications, passport markings.

Now

A patchwork — and an ongoing legal debate

Each state has different rules. Courts in multiple circuits are actively questioning whether the current system remains constitutional.

Section 03 — Behavioral Science

What does the research say about risk?

Risk of reoffense is not fixed — research consistently shows it decreases over time for most people.

Long-term studies consistently find that the majority of people convicted of sexual offenses do not commit new sexual offenses after release. Recidivism rates vary by how "recidivism" is defined — a critical distinction — but studies measuring new sexual convictions typically find rates in the range of 5 to 15 percent over five-to-fifteen-year follow-up periods.

Perhaps more importantly, risk is not static. Multiple large studies have found that the longer a person remains offense-free, the lower their risk of future offending becomes. This is called desistance — and it has significant implications for lifetime registration policies.

The structural limitation: Because registries only list people already convicted, they cannot include first-time offenders — responsible for the majority of new sexual offenses. This is a factual constraint that researchers consistently note when evaluating registry effectiveness.

Section 04 — Collateral Impact

Impact on families and communities

Children and spouses of registrants often bear significant collateral consequences they did not cause.

Registration doesn't affect only the person convicted. Research on collateral consequences is growing — and what it finds is sobering. Residency restrictions can force entire families to relocate. Public registry listings affect employment, housing, and community relationships for everyone who shares a home with a registrant.

Children of registrants often face social consequences at school when their parent's name appears on a public website. Spouses lose housing options, sometimes income, and regularly face social stigma for a conviction they had no part in.

Residency restrictions in many states make it mathematically impossible for a registrant to find legal housing in densely populated areas — pushing families toward rural isolation or homelessness.

Section 05 — Understanding the Data

Paperwork violations vs. new crimes

When a registered person is arrested, it is often reported simply as a sex offender "reoffending." But there are several very different things that can trigger an arrest — and they are not remotely equivalent.

In most states, failing to update an address, forgetting to report a new vehicle, spending a night away from home without notifying authorities, or failing to report a new email address are all criminal offenses. These administrative violations are counted alongside new sexual assault allegations in many "recidivism" statistics.

When a registrant is arrested, it could be for either of these — and they are not the same thing

Administrative / Technical Violation
  • Failed to update address in time
  • Did not report a new vehicle
  • Spent a night away without reporting
  • Missed an in-person check-in
  • Did not report a new email or username
  • Travel not reported in advance

New Sexual Offense Allegation
  • Allegation of a new sexual assault
  • New abuse allegation
  • New contact offense

Both are crimes under state law. The point is that combining them in a single recidivism statistic tells us something very different than separating them — and that difference matters enormously for honest policy evaluation.

Court Decisions

The legal landscape — explained plainly

Federal courts in multiple circuits are reconsidering whether expanded registry requirements remain constitutional.

The foundational case is Smith v. Doe (2003), in which the Supreme Court ruled that Alaska's registry was civil regulation rather than criminal punishment — allowing it to apply retroactively. That ruling shaped every legal challenge since.

But as requirements have expanded dramatically beyond what existed in 2003, courts are increasingly asking whether the original analysis still holds. The central legal question: has the registry crossed the line from regulation into punishment?

The key question in courts today: Has the registry changed enough since 2003 that the original Supreme Court ruling no longer covers what states are actually doing?

Smith v. Doe
U.S. Supreme Court · 2003

The foundational case. The Court ruled Alaska's registry was civil regulation, not punishment — allowing retroactive application. But the registry reviewed in 2003 was far less expansive than what most states require today.

Doe v. Snyder
6th Circuit Court of Appeals · 2016

A landmark departure. The court ruled Michigan's expanded requirements had become so burdensome they constituted punishment — and could not apply to people convicted before those rules existed.

State v. Letalvis Cofield
North Carolina Supreme Court · 2023

North Carolina's highest court struck down satellite-based monitoring for some registrants as an unreasonable search under the Fourth Amendment. Courts are scrutinizing modern surveillance tools.

Does v. Wasden & others
Multiple federal circuits · Ongoing

Challenges to expanded internet reporting, travel restrictions, and residency requirements are working through federal courts — arguing the system has grown beyond what any court has approved.

Common Questions

Questions people actually search for

These are the questions families, registrants, journalists, students, and curious citizens most often ask. Answered as plainly and honestly as the research allows.

Does the sex offender registry actually make communities safer? +

The research on this is genuinely mixed. Some studies find modest public safety benefits in certain contexts. Others find little to no measurable reduction in sexual offending linked to registry laws. What researchers broadly agree on is that the evidence is not as clear-cut as the policy assumes — and that the system has grown significantly since it was first evaluated.

Do sex offenders always reoffend? +

No — and this is one of the most persistent myths the research pushes back on. Long-term studies consistently find that most people convicted of sexual offenses do not commit new sexual offenses after release. Risk also decreases significantly over time — a phenomenon called desistance.

Can someone get off the sex offender registry? +

It depends entirely on the state. Some states have petition processes allowing people to apply for removal after a certain number of offense-free years. Others have lifetime registration with no removal option. Federal law sets minimum standards but states can be more restrictive. An attorney familiar with your state's laws is the right resource for individual situations.

What happens if someone misses a reporting deadline? +

Missing a reporting requirement — even something like failing to report a new email address or an overnight stay away from home — can result in a new criminal charge in most states. These administrative violations are real crimes under state law, but they are paperwork offenses, not new sexual assaults. This distinction matters enormously when evaluating registry statistics.

Most abuse is committed by strangers, right? +

Research consistently shows the opposite. The majority of sexual offenses, particularly against children, are committed by someone the victim already knows — a family member, trusted adult, or acquaintance. The registry's public notification model is largely built around stranger danger, which research suggests does not reflect how most sexual harm actually occurs.

Is the registry the same in every state? +

No. Requirements vary significantly. Some states require quarterly in-person reporting; others annual. Some have residency restrictions within 2,500 feet of schools; others have none. Duration ranges from 10 years to lifetime. Federal law sets a minimum floor, but states can and do exceed it substantially.

How does registration affect the children of registrants? +

Children of registrants often face social consequences at school, loss of housing stability when residency restrictions force relocation, and stigma from a public registry listing they had no part in creating. Research on these collateral effects is growing and increasingly part of the policy conversation.

Why is this website not advocacy? +

Because the evidence doesn't belong to one side. This site follows the same principle as the Registry Research Institute: evidence before opinion. We're not arguing for or against the registry. We're explaining what the research shows so you can form your own informed view. Where science is mixed or uncertain, we say so clearly.

The greatest threat to public safety is not knowledge, but ignorance.

— Justice Louis D. Brandeis, U.S. Supreme Court

Who is behind RegistryFacts.org?

This site is a public education resource produced in partnership with the Florida Action Foundation — a Florida-based organization dedicated to evidence-based reform of sex offender registration laws through public education and strategic litigation.
